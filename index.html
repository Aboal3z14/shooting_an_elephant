01

External Hostility & Colonial Resentment

Explanation
Orwell describes how the Burmese people strongly dislike Europeans, especially him as a police officer. He is insulted, mocked, and humiliated— laying bare the raw tension between colonizers and the colonised.
Themes
Hatred caused by imperialism Conflict between rulers and the ruled
Figures of Speech & Devices
Imagery "sneering yellow faces"
Hyperbole "large numbers of people"
Tone Bitter and frustrated
02

Inner Conflict & Moral Confusion

Explanation
Orwell reveals his inner turmoil — he despises the machinery of empire, yet simultaneously nurses anger toward the Burmese. He is trapped between two irreconcilable positions, belonging fully to neither.
Themes
Inner conflict Moral confusion under imperialism
Figures of Speech & Devices
Contrast Hatred of empire vs. anger at the Burmese
Metaphor "dirty work of Empire"
Strong Diction "evil," "oppressors," "guilt"
03

Catalytic Action & Symbolic Power

Explanation
Orwell introduces the rampaging elephant — the inciting event that sets the essay's moral machinery in motion. The destruction it causes forces Orwell into a public test of authority and conscience.
Themes
Power and responsibility Moral testing
Figures of Speech & Devices
Foreshadowing "enlightening"
Imagery Vivid destruction caused by elephant
Symbolism Elephant as a vessel of imperial power

Narrative Progression

P1

External Conflict

The world pushes back against Orwell. Hostility, mockery, and the visible hatred of the colonised make the weight of empire impossible to ignore.

P2

Internal Conflict

The battle moves inward. Orwell wrestles with his own complicity — hating imperialism while fearing and resenting those he oppresses.

P3

Catalyst & Action

The elephant shatters the stasis. External event meets internal paralysis, forcing a moral reckoning that will define the entire essay.

"When the white man turns tyrant it is his own freedom that he destroys."

George Orwell — Shooting an Elephant
